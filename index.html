Frequently Asked Questions

According to our research, 57% of U.S. undergraduates now rely on AI assistants for coursework, with 65% using chatbots like ChatGPT at least weekly. This represents a significant increase from just one-third of students two years ago.

ChatGPT leads with 66% of student users, followed by Grammarly (AI mode) and Microsoft Copilot at 25% each. Claude AI and GitHub Copilot round out the top 5 with 12% and 11% respectively.

Students report a 34% reduction in time-on-task for introductory writing assignments. The average time to complete a 1,500-word paper has fallen from 9.7 hours in 2022 to 6.2 hours in 2025 with AI assistance.

Only 28% of U.S. institutions currently have formal College AI policies in place, while another 32% are developing one. This policy gap creates confusion, with 31% of students unclear on permissible AI use in their coursework.

The primary challenges include prompt-engineering anxiety (48% not confident), policy confusion (31% unclear on permissible use), and concerns about academic integrity. Many students struggle with knowing how to effectively communicate with AI tools.

Business majors lead AI adoption at 62%, slightly ahead of STEM students at 59%. Humanities students follow at 52%. The gap suggests varying comfort levels and perceived utility across different fields of study.

AI literacy is expected to become a measured learning outcome at nearly 39% of institutions within three years. We anticipate a growing skills divide between AI-fluent students and non-users, making AI literacy increasingly important for academic and career success.

Educators should integrate AI policies into syllabi, embed AI literacy modules in first-year seminars, and focus on assessing process rather than just final products. Clear guidelines paired with robust AI literacy programs will help students use AI ethically and effectively.
